Quality Improvement Case Study

Project Overview

Reducing Hospital Readmissions Through Better Discharge Management

Hospital readmissions increase costs, strain healthcare resources, and can indicate opportunities to improve care transitions. This project analyzed more than 100,000 hospital encounters to identify factors associated with elevated readmission risk and recommend operational improvements that could improve patient outcomes.

The Problem

Hospital readmissions create financial, operational, and patient care challenges. Reducing avoidable readmissions improves outcomes, lowers costs, and increases healthcare system efficiency.

The goal of this project was to identify factors associated with elevated readmission risk and uncover opportunities for intervention that healthcare organizations could realistically implement.

Why It Matters

Healthcare organizations face increasing pressure to improve quality metrics while controlling costs. Understanding the drivers of readmission can help providers prioritize resources, improve discharge planning, and deliver better patient outcomes.

Data Considerations

Approximately 50% of referral specialist records were missing specialty classifications. To preserve the integrity of specialty-level comparisons, missing values were categorized as "Unknown" and analyzed separately rather than excluded from the dataset.

Investigation

To better understand readmission risk, I evaluated patient encounters across diagnosis categories, referral specialties, comorbidities, and medication management practices.

The analysis focused on identifying factors associated with elevated readmission rates while prioritizing findings that could realistically be addressed through operational or clinical workflow improvements.

Key Findings

  • Overall hospital readmission rate was 11.2%.
  • Patients diagnosed with diabetes experienced a higher readmission rate of 13.1%.
  • Diabetic patients discharged without medication changes demonstrated the highest readmission rate at 15%.
  • Patients referred through Cardiology demonstrated a significantly lower readmission rate of 8%.

Recommendations

Implement a mandatory medication review process for diabetic patients prior to discharge, particularly for individuals whose medication regimens remained unchanged during hospitalization.

Evaluate Cardiology discharge and follow-up protocols for adoption across additional specialties. Since Cardiology consistently achieved lower readmission rates, its care coordination practices may provide a useful model for improving outcomes elsewhere in the organization.

Potential Impacts

The findings suggest that targeted medication review processes and broader adoption of successful discharge practices could reduce avoidable readmissions, improve patient outcomes, and lower operational costs.

What I Learned

This project reinforced the importance of focusing on actionable opportunities rather than simply identifying statistical relationships. The most valuable findings were not necessarily the strongest correlations, but the factors healthcare organizations could realistically influence through workflow improvements and discharge planning processes.
